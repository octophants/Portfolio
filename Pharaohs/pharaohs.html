Pharaohs

A static data visualization piece which organizes multiple streams of complex quantitative and qualitative data into separate layers and simultaneously compiles them into one, solid, comprehensive package of information.

The original piece measures 96 x 142 inches, but it has been designed for responsive viewability on any and every device. The text, graphs and other elements dynamically resize themselves to suit the user's screen size. The information always appears precise, clear and pristine.

How do you document 3000 years of historical information from an ancient civilization...

...and present it to the public in a visually stimulating and comprehensive manner?

I filed my collected data in a spreadsheet to sort out saturated information into individual layers –

  1. Names of all 250 pharaohs.
  2. Duration of ruling period.
  3. Gender of each ruler.
  4. Relationship to predecessor/successor (child, parent, spouse).
  5. Burial location of each ruler.
  6. Dynasty/Family groupings.
  7. Historical time periods.
  8. Influence of each dynasty, depending on duration of reign.

1.
Names

Of all the known rulers since 3150 BC. They can be as long as Hotepsekhemwy, or as short as Ay.

2.
REIGN

The reigning period of each king/queen. Some ruled for fifty years, others sat on the throne for less than a year. The arched lines mark the ruling periods on the time circle, which extends from 3200 BC to 30 BC.

3.
GENDER

Most of the pharaohs were unsurprisingly male, but some of the most prominent and powerful rulers (like Hatshepsut and Cleopatra) were queens. Blue dots represent males, while pink represent females.

4.
RELATIONS

It’s a well known fact that Egyptians were known to be incestuous, marrying their siblings in order to preserve the pure royal bloodline. Circles represent parent-offspring relationships, straight lines show sibling relationships, and the crossed lines show marriages.

This complicated setup shows that Ptolemy V had three children – Ptolemy VI, Cleopatra II and Ptolemy VIII, and Cleopatra ended up marrying both her brothers.

5.
BURIAL

Members of the same family/ dynasty were usually buried in the same tomb locations. The colored dots and lines represent the burial locations of each ruler on the map of present day Egypt.

6.
DYNASTY

A dynasty constituted members of a certain bloodline, so all pharaohs of a particular dynasty were related in some way.

7.
HISTORY

The spans of dynasties coincided with certain historical periods, representing changes in the socio-political and cultural atmospheres of the time.

8.
INFLUENCE

The rings show the influence of each family bloodline based on the number of years they were in power. Each ring represents 10 years. The most powerful families are hence shown to hold the largest ring circles.
